**Nectar Collection**
Forager bees collect nectar from flowering plants and store it in their crop, also called the "honey stomach," without digesting it.

**Bee-to-Bee Transfer**
The forager regurgitates nectar directly into a processor bee's crop at or near the hive entrance. The processor then carries this nectar to the honeycomb.

**Enzymatic Conversion**
During the ripening process, processor bees add an enzyme called invertase each time they regurgitate. This enzyme "breaks the sucrose down into two simpler sugars: glucose (blood sugar) and fructose (fruit sugar)."

**Dehydration**
Water originally comprises approximately 70 percent of nectar, but honey must contain less than 18.6 percent water. Bees reduce moisture by "fanning their wings, which creates airflow around the honeycomb and helps water evaporate."

**Sealing**
Once ripened, processor bees "cap" each cell with an airtight wax seal. This prevents moisture absorption from air and protects against bacterial or fungal contamination, since microbes cannot survive in the low-moisture environment.
