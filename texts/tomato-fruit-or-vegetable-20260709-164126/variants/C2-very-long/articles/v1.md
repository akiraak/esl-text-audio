# Is a Tomato a Fruit or a Vegetable?

Few questions sound as harmless as this one, and fewer still turn out to be quite so quietly treacherous. Put it to a room full of people — is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? — and most will answer at once, with the untroubled confidence of someone who has never had the faintest reason to doubt it. And yet the honest reply is not a word at all but a counter-question: who, precisely, is doing the asking? For a scientist and a cook will each examine the very same tomato and arrive at flatly opposite verdicts, and — here is the part that tends to unsettle people — both of them will be entirely, demonstrably right. What follows is the story of one small red thing, of the two truths that decline to cancel each other out, of the whole company of familiar foods that lead the same quiet double life, and of the improbable afternoon on which the disagreement was carried all the way to the Supreme Court of the United States.

## It Depends on Who You Ask

The trouble with the question is that it disguises its own difficulty. It has the shape of the sort of thing that ought to have a single, settled answer — the kind you might find printed upside down at the foot of a quiz page. Is a whale a fish? No. Is a spider an insect? No. Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? Here the machinery jams, because unlike those tidy cases the tomato does not sit on one side of a single line. It sits, quite comfortably, on both sides of two entirely different lines, drawn by two different kinds of expert, for two entirely different purposes.

This is precisely why the confident answers come so quickly and yet so often collide. The person who says "vegetable, obviously" is picturing dinner — a salad, a sauce, a slice on a sandwich. The person who says "fruit, actually" is retrieving a half-buried fact from a school biology lesson. Neither is wrong; but neither has noticed that they are answering different questions, and so each is faintly astonished to be contradicted. The genuine interest of the tomato is not that it is hard to classify but that it is easy to classify twice, in two seemingly incompatible ways, each of them impeccably correct within its own world.

We would do well, then, to resist the instinct to force a single verdict. The question "which is it, really?" quietly assumes that one of the two answers must be the true one and the other a popular mistake — that behind the confusion there lies a single fact of the matter waiting to be uncovered. The whole burden of this essay is that no such single fact exists, and that the search for it is precisely the wrong way to understand the tomato. To see why, we must look with some patience at both worlds in turn: first the world of the scientist, with its flowers and seeds and reproductive machinery, and then the world of the cook, with its flavours and its dishes and its wholly different set of concerns.

## Two Ways to Sort Food

Before we descend into either world, it is worth pausing on a point that the argument usually rushes past. There is no law of nature that says food must be sorted in only one way. We are so accustomed to a single set of familiar categories — fruit, vegetable, meat, grain — that we imagine these labels are simply read off the things themselves, as though every cucumber came into the world with an invisible tag already attached. But a category is not a property of an object; it is a tool that somebody built for a purpose. And when the purposes differ, the tools will differ too, and the same object may find itself sorted into different boxes without anything about the object having changed in the slightest.

Consider how many different ways a single kitchen might sort its contents. You could group foods by where they are stored — the cupboard, the refrigerator, the freezer. You could group them by how long they keep, or by how much they cost, or by which meal they belong to, or by whether a particular guest is able to eat them. Each of these schemes is perfectly sensible; each answers a real question; and no two of them need agree. The refrigerator does not care whether the tomato grew from a flower, and the botanist does not care whether it needs to be kept cold. They are simply not in the same conversation.

The two classifications that concern us here — the botanical and the culinary — are exactly this sort of pair. One was built by scientists to understand how plants grow and reproduce and how they are related to one another. The other was built, over centuries and without anyone in particular designing it, by everybody who has ever had to plan a meal, shop for ingredients, or advise another person on what to eat. The first serves the laboratory; the second serves the general public, the nutritionist, and the chef. They were made for different work, and so it should surprise no one — though somehow it always does — that they occasionally place the same food in different provinces.

It is a mistake, and a surprisingly common one, to imagine that in any such pair one classification must be the "real" one and the other a convenient approximation. People tend to assume that the scientific scheme is the truth and the everyday scheme a rough-and-ready shorthand — that the botanist knows what the tomato *really* is, while the cook merely knows how it is loosely spoken of. But this gets the situation exactly wrong. Neither scheme is a blurred version of the other; each is sharp and exact within its own purpose, and hopelessly beside the point outside it. The botanical definition is the truth about how the tomato grows; the culinary definition is the truth about how the tomato is eaten. There is no further, deeper truth hovering above them both, waiting to declare a winner. Keep this firmly in mind and the whole apparent paradox of the tomato begins, gently, to dissolve.

## What Science Calls a Fruit

Begin with science, since science at least promises to be tidy. Botany, the study of plants, takes no interest in what tastes agreeable or what belongs beside the roast. It asks instead where a thing comes from and what it is built to do. To a botanist, a fruit is the seed-bearing structure that develops from the ovary of a flowering plant — the part that grows out of the flower and carries the seeds within it. Its purpose is reproductive rather than gastronomic: it exists to shelter the seeds and, in the fullness of time, to help the parent plant scatter its offspring into the world.

Observe what this definition conspicuously declines to mention. It says nothing whatever about sweetness. It does not ask whether you would gladly eat the thing for pudding, nor whether it would look eccentric in a salad. Sugar, flavour, the company a food keeps at the table — all of this lies entirely outside the botanist's field of vision. The single question that counts is one of origin and structure: did it grow from a flower, and does it enclose seeds? If the answer is yes, then whatever else the thing may be, science files it — without hesitation and without apology — under *fruit*.

There is a sound reason the botanist insists on so narrow and unsentimental a rule. Classification, for the scientist, is not a game of intuition but an instrument for understanding how plants are related, how they grow, and how they reproduce; it helps in identifying varieties and in grasping the biology of the plant itself. A definition anchored to something as fickle as taste would be useless for that work. Sweetness varies with ripeness, with variety, with the season, and with the soil; it tells you nothing dependable about a plant's biology. Anchor the definition instead to the flower and the seed, and you gain something stable to reason with — something that holds firm across every kitchen and every cuisine on earth, and that lets a botanist in one country understand exactly what a botanist in another is talking about. Precision of this kind is not pedantry; it is the very thing that makes the science work.

## A Closer Look at the Tomato

Hold the tomato up to that rule and the verdict all but delivers itself. A tomato plant produces small yellow flowers, and it is precisely from these flowers that each tomato swells and reddens on the vine; the fruit grows directly out of the bloom, exactly as the definition requires. There is no intermediate step to explain away, no awkward gap between flower and food. The flower is, quite literally, where the tomato begins.

Slice one open and the case grows harder still to dispute. Inside you find not a single seed but a whole crowded multitude of them, bedded in their soft, wet chambers of pulp, every one a potential plant biding its time. This is not incidental to what a tomato is; it is the whole point of it, from the plant's own perspective. The juicy flesh that we prize is, in the plant's design, merely the packaging and the bribe — the sweet, conspicuous parcel that persuades an animal to carry the seeds away and deposit them, conveniently fertilised, somewhere new.

Seen in this light, the very qualities that tempt us to eat the tomato are the plant's own recruitment of us to its purposes. The colour that catches the eye, the moisture, the faint sweetness — all of it is bait, evolved over countless generations to secure exactly the outcome the plant requires: that some creature should find the thing worth eating and, in the eating, scatter the seeds abroad. From the plant's side of the bargain, the tomato is not food at all but a clever device for reproduction, and the botanist's insistence on classing it as a fruit simply honours that fact. The definition tracks what the structure is *for* in the life of the plant, which is why it can afford to ignore, so completely, what the structure tastes like to us.

So the tomato honours every clause of the botanical definition, and it does so without a flicker of ambiguity. It springs from a flower; it harbours its many seeds; it is manifestly the seed-bearing product of a flowering plant. For a scientist, then, there is no genuine riddle here at all — no delicate borderline case to be argued long into the night, of the sort that does sometimes trouble the edges of botanical classification. Measured against the rule, the tomato is a fruit: plainly, structurally, and beyond any reasonable contradiction. Were that the whole of the matter, the tomato would scarcely deserve an essay. It is not the whole of the matter, and the reason it is not takes us out of the laboratory altogether and into a place where the botanist's beautiful, stable rule turns out to answer none of the questions anyone is actually asking.

## How Cooks and Shoppers Think

Step out of the laboratory and into the kitchen and the entire frame of the question shifts silently beneath your feet. Cooks are not indifferent to where their food comes from, but origin is simply not the axis on which their world turns. In the kitchen, food is sorted not by flower and seed but by flavour and by use — by how a thing actually conducts itself once it meets the knife, the heat, and the palate. What matters is taste and function: is this something sweet, to be dwelt upon at the end of a meal as a dessert, or something savoury, to be built into the substantial part of it?

This is no arbitrary prejudice, no mere neglect of the biology textbook. The culinary way of sorting food earns its keep precisely because it answers questions the botanical scheme cannot so much as hear. It is the classification that genuinely serves chefs, shoppers, and nutritionists, and it serves them for a concrete reason: foods that are close relations in botanical terms do not necessarily share anything at all once they reach the plate. Two plants may be near cousins in the flower and yet possess utterly different flavours, different uses, and — this is the point the nutritionist cares about — different nutritional makeups, so that knowing their botanical kinship tells you next to nothing about how to eat them or what they will do for your body.

When you are deciding what to cook, or what to carry home from the market, or how to advise someone on a balanced diet, a common ancestry among flowers is of no earthly use to you. What you need to know is how the thing tastes, what can be done with it, and what part it might play in a meal. On precisely these questions botany falls mute, and the culinary classification steps forward to answer.

Picture the practical business of building a menu, and the point becomes vivid. A cook laying out the courses of a dinner is thinking about balance, sequence, and contrast — something savoury to anchor the meal, something sweet to close it, something sharp to wake the palate in between. Within that scheme the tomato belongs, unmistakably, among the savoury anchors: it goes into the salad that opens the meal or the sauce that clothes the main course, and no cook in their senses would send it out at the end, sugared, as the pudding. That the tomato happens to grow from a flower and cradle its seeds is, for this purpose, a fact of supreme irrelevance. The menu does not care about the flower; the menu cares about the meal. And so the culinary scheme, far from being a lazy approximation of the botanical one, is simply a different instrument tuned to a different task.

It is worth insisting, too, that this sorting is not the crude or unschooled cousin of the scientific one. It has its own logic, its own long tradition, and its own hard-won expertise. A chef who instinctively groups the tomato with the onion rather than the apple is making a judgement every bit as principled, within its own domain, as the botanist who does the reverse. The two are not rivals contending for a single crown; they are specialists tending separate kingdoms, each sovereign within its own borders and each faintly baffled by the other's obsessions.

## The Tomato in the Kitchen

And by the cook's measure, the tomato settles firmly among the vegetables. It can, to be sure, be juicy and even mildly sweet, and nothing on earth forbids you from eating one raw, out of the hand, as you might an apple in the garden. But that is not how the tomato earns its living, and the occasional raw tomato does not overturn the general rule of its working life. In everyday practice the tomato is put to savoury work — sliced into salads, simmered into soups, cooked slowly down into sauces, roasted, stuffed, stewed, and set alongside the onions and the peppers and all the other undisputed vegetables of the pot. It is a labourer in the main course, not the sweet flourish that closes the meal.

That, in the end, is why cooks, shoppers, and countless ordinary eaters have always called the tomato a vegetable and felt not the faintest twinge of error in doing so. They are not being careless, nor are they the victims of some fact that would embarrass them if only they knew it. They are simply answering a different question from the botanist's. Where science asks how the tomato is *made*, the kitchen asks how the tomato is *used*; and by that wholly reasonable standard the tomato is a vegetable, exactly as surely as it is a fruit by the other.

Notice, too, how little the botanical fact actually changes about the way anyone behaves. A person who learns, perhaps for the first time, that the tomato is "really" a fruit does not thereupon begin serving it with cream after dinner, or slicing it into a bowl of berries. The revelation, however delightful, has no consequences for the plate; the tomato goes on being chopped into the salad exactly as before. This is worth dwelling on, because it shows that the culinary classification is not a mistake waiting to be corrected by better science. If it were merely an error, knowing the truth would fix it. But knowing the botanical truth leaves the kitchen entirely untouched — which is the surest possible sign that the kitchen was answering a different question all along, one that the botany was never in a position to settle.

There is a useful way to feel the force of this. Imagine two people standing over the same bowl of tomatoes, each perfectly informed, each perfectly sincere, and each flatly contradicting the other — one saying "fruit," the other "vegetable" — and imagine, further, that neither can be talked out of their answer by any fact the other might produce. The botanist can dissect the tomato, point to the flower and the seeds, and prove beyond dispute that it is a fruit; and the cook can grant every word of it and still be entirely right to call it a vegetable, because the cook was never denying the botany in the first place. The disagreement is real on the surface and unreal underneath, like two people arguing about whether it is "morning" while standing in different time zones. Everything depends on the frame, and the frame is set by the question.

Here, then, is the heart of the whole affair, stated as plainly as it can be. The two answers do not compete. They do not even truly meet. They occupy different rooms in the same house — the laboratory and the kitchen — and there is no staircase between the rooms on which the two verdicts might collide and quarrel. "Fruit" answers the question *what is this, and where did it come from?* "Vegetable" answers the question *what is this for, and how shall I use it?* Both questions are worth asking; both have correct answers; and the answers differ only because the questions do.

## Tomato Is Not Alone

It is tempting to suppose that the tomato is some singular oddity, a lone troublemaker hauled out to make a clever debating point. It is nothing of the kind. The same amiable double life is led by a whole company of thoroughly familiar foods, and once you know to look for them you begin to see them everywhere on the plate. Cucumbers, squash, peppers, aubergines, green peas, and sweetcorn are every one of them fruits by the botanical reckoning — each grown from a flower and stocked with seeds — and yet each is treated, without a moment's hesitation, as a vegetable in the kitchen.

The tomato, in other words, is merely the most celebrated member of a large and entirely respectable club — the one whose name comes up first whenever the argument is raised, not because it is exceptional but because it is famous. Slice open a cucumber and you will find the seeds; look at the flower from which a squash swells and you will see the same botanical story unfolding as with the tomato. The pepper, the aubergine, the pea in its pod: all of them are, to the botanist, seed-bearing products of a flower, and all of them are, to the cook, plainly vegetables, chopped and salted and served in savoury company without a second thought given to their flowery origins.

Nor is the list a short one. To the cucumber, the squash, the pepper, the aubergine, the green pea, and the sweetcorn one might add such everyday items as the courgette, the olive, and the avocado — all of them, on the botanical reckoning, fruits, and all of them treated as savoury foods in the ordinary run of cooking. The sheer length of the roll-call is itself the lesson. If a single food led this double life, we might dismiss it as a curiosity; when a whole crowd of the most ordinary items on the shelf does the same, the double life is revealed as the normal condition rather than the exception. The wonder is not that the tomato is claimed by two classifications at once, but that we ever expected any of these foods to belong tidily to just one.

What this multitude teaches is that the tomato's double life is not a freak but a pattern — the entirely predictable consequence of running two honest classification systems side by side. Wherever the botanical map and the culinary map are laid over the same territory, there will be towns that the two maps assign to different provinces, and the tomato is only the best-known of these divided towns. It is not that the systems have gone wrong. It is that they were never trying to agree, and a whole crowd of ordinary vegetables quietly proves the point every day.

## Sometimes It Works the Other Way

If the border between fruit and vegetable were a one-way gate — botanical fruits wandering over to be treated as vegetables, and never the reverse — one might still suspect some lurking asymmetry, some sense in which "vegetable" was the softer, less rigorous category into which stray fruits drifted. But the traffic, intriguingly, runs in both directions, and this two-way movement is what finally shows the border for what it is.

Consider rhubarb, and consider the sweet potato. Both are vegetables by strict botanical classification — neither is the seed-bearing product of a flower in the way a tomato is — and yet both are cheerfully enlisted in sweet, dessert-style recipes. Rhubarb is stewed with sugar until it collapses into a tart pink compote and folded into pies and crumbles, taking its place at the pudding end of the meal as though it had every right to be there. The sweet potato is candied, spiced, whipped into sweet pies, and served in dishes that would look entirely at home beside the desserts, despite its being, to the botanist, no fruit at all.

Here, then, are the perfect mirror-images of the tomato. The tomato is a botanical fruit that the kitchen treats as a vegetable; rhubarb and the sweet potato are botanical vegetables that the kitchen is happy to treat, on occasion, as something close to fruit. Set the two cases side by side and the symmetry is complete: the border between the categories is crossed in both directions, by respectable foods, without anyone raising an objection. If the culinary scheme were merely a sloppy shadow of the botanical one, we should expect the errors to run only one way; instead they run both ways, which is precisely what we should expect not of errors at all but of two independent schemes, each following its own logic wherever that logic happens to lead.

So the border traffic flows both ways: botanical fruits doing the daily work of vegetables, and botanical vegetables occasionally moonlighting as sweets. Once you have grasped that there are two honest systems of classification rather than one master scheme with a few awkward exceptions, this two-way movement stops looking like a paradox and starts looking like exactly what you should have predicted. Different maps, drawn for different journeys, will inevitably place some of the same towns in different provinces — and some towns will even seem to migrate, depending on which map you happen to be holding. The tomato is not an anomaly in an otherwise orderly world. It is a particularly loud example of how the world was always going to look, the moment we agreed to describe it in more than one useful way at once.

## When It Became About Money

Here the tale takes its most improbable turn, for this genial disagreement did not stay confined to dinner tables and biology classes, where it might have remained a pleasant puzzle forever. In the United States it acquired, quite suddenly, a hard financial edge — because the question of whether a tomato was a fruit or a vegetable turned out to be worth actual money, and once money is involved, no question stays academic for long.

The instrument that transformed the puzzle into a dispute was the Tariff Act of 1883, signed into law by President Chester A. Arthur on the third of March that year. The Act required a tax to be paid on imported vegetables, while allowing imported fruit to enter the country entirely duty-free. On its own, this was an unremarkable piece of tax law, of the kind that governments produce by the yard. But laid across the peculiar double nature of the tomato, it became something close to a trap. For if the tomato was a fruit, it could be imported without the tax; and if it was a vegetable, the importer had to pay. The airy classroom question — fruit or vegetable? — had grown, overnight, a price tag attached to every crate that crossed the border.

It did not take long for someone to notice the opportunity, or the grievance, depending on which way you looked at it. A family of importers named Nix, who brought tomatoes through the busy port of New York, had every financial reason to insist that their tomatoes were fruit and therefore owed no duty. To the tax collector at the port, of course, they were vegetables, and the money was due. Between these two positions there was no room for a genial "it depends on who you ask"; the customs house needed a single answer, in dollars and cents, and it needed the law to supply it.

This is the crucial thing that the courtroom did to the question, and it is what makes the case so instructive. Everywhere else — at the dinner table, in the classroom, in idle argument among friends — the tomato is permitted its comfortable double life, and nobody is the worse for it. The law, however, cannot tolerate two answers. A tariff must be either paid or not paid; a crate of tomatoes cannot be taxed as a vegetable and waved through as a fruit at the same moment. The legal setting forced the two peaceable classifications, which had coexisted for centuries without friction, into a genuine collision from which only one could walk away. The tomato's charming ambiguity, harmless in the kitchen, became intolerable at the customs house, and something had to give.

And so a matter of pennies per crate began its unlikely ascent through the American courts, carrying with it the whole ancient argument about flowers and seeds and savoury dishes, now dressed up in the sober language of tariffs and law. It is a fine measure of how a trivial-seeming question can acquire real weight the instant it is attached to money and made to answer for itself before a judge.

## The Court Decides

The Nix brothers sued Edward L. Hedden, the collector of the port of New York, and the case climbed, with a certain absurd dignity, all the way to the Supreme Court of the United States. There, on the tenth of May in 1893, it reached its conclusion in an opinion written, for a unanimous Court, by Justice Horace Gray — and the reasoning he set down is a small monument to common sense, all the more charming for the solemnity of its setting.

Gray began by conceding the botanists everything. The passages cited from the dictionaries, he acknowledged, defined the word "fruit" as the seed of plants — which is to say that, on the strict botanical definition, the tomato's champions had their facts in perfect order. He did not dispute the science for a moment. But those definitions, he observed, had no tendency to show that tomatoes were "fruit," as distinguished from "vegetables," in the common speech of everyday life. And it was common speech, not the botany textbook, that the tariff law had been written in and was plainly meant to be read by. In ordinary language, tomatoes were served at dinner as part of the main course — in, or with, the soup, the fish, or the meat — and not, like fruits generally, as dessert. On that homely and entirely practical basis, the Court held unanimously that tomatoes were to be classed as vegetables for the purposes of the tariff, and the tax was owed.

There is a fine irony buried in the argument, and it is worth drawing out. The importers lost precisely because they were, botanically, correct. They came to court armed with the true and unassailable fact that a tomato is a fruit, and the Court did not contradict them on that point at all; it simply ruled that the fact, however true, was the answer to the wrong question. The tariff had been written by ordinary people in ordinary language, for the ordinary purposes of trade, and in that language the tomato was a vegetable. To insist on the botanical meaning of the word in a statute plainly built around the culinary one was, the Court in effect held, to misread the law by being too clever about the science. Rarely has being right availed a litigant so little.

Nor did the Court stop with the tomato. Justice Gray extended the same plain reasoning to a whole cluster of related foods — cucumbers, squashes, peas, and beans — all of which the everyday tongue likewise counted among the vegetables, whatever the botanists in their laboratories might strictly prefer. In a single stroke he brought the law into line with the kitchen, and the tomato's savoury companions came along with it. The very foods we met earlier as members of the tomato's double-living club now reappeared, quite fittingly, in the pages of a Supreme Court report, sorted by the judges exactly as they are sorted at the dinner table.

What deserves the closest attention, however, is what the Court carefully refrained from doing. It did not presume to overturn the science. It did not declare that the tomato had somehow ceased, in nature, to be a fruit, and Gray's opinion is at pains to concede the botanical definition rather than deny it. The Court simply decided that, for the narrow legal question actually before it, the right authority was common usage rather than botanical strictness. The justices, robed and unanimous, thus placed the full weight of the law behind precisely the truce that this whole story has been circling toward — a formal, black-letter acknowledgement that the tomato may be one thing to the scientist and quite another to everyone standing in a grocery queue, and that both may be perfectly right at the same time.

That, surely, is why the case is remembered at all, long after the particular crates of tomatoes have rotted and the pennies of duty have been forgotten. It endures not as a landmark of tax law but as a small, near-perfect parable. A pair of importers went to the highest court in the land clutching an unimpeachable scientific fact; the court agreed with the fact and ruled against them anyway; and in doing so it stated, with all the gravity of the bench, a truth that any thoughtful cook could have supplied for nothing — that words take their meaning from the purposes they serve, and that the tomato answers to two masters without betraying either. It is not every day that the machinery of a national government is set in motion to settle, once and for all, a question that turns out to have two correct answers.

## Both Answers Are Right

So we arrive, by way of yellow flowers and crowded seeds, of simmering sauces and stewed rhubarb, of a nineteenth-century tax and a unanimous bench of judges, at the only conclusion the evidence will support. We began with a question that seemed to demand a single word for an answer, and we have found instead that the single word was hiding two, each of them true. The tomato is a fruit to the botanist and a vegetable to the cook, and neither answer is a forgery of the other. They are two correct answers to two genuinely different questions — one about how the tomato comes into being, the other about how it is eaten — and no amount of argument, however patient or ingenious, will ever collapse them into a single tidy verdict, because the two questions were never asking the same thing to begin with.

There is something quietly liberating in accepting this. We are trained, almost from infancy, to believe that every question of fact has exactly one right answer, and that anyone who insists on two must be fudging or hedging or trying to have it both ways. The tomato gently disproves the rule. It demonstrates that a thing can belong, wholeheartedly and without contradiction, to two categories at once, provided only that the categories were built for different kinds of work — and that the whole appearance of paradox can dissolve the instant you stop asking "which is it, really?" and start asking, of each classification in turn, "what is this one *for*?"

The habit of mind is worth carrying beyond the greengrocer's. A great many of the disputes that feel, from the inside, like flat contradictions turn out on inspection to be exactly like the tomato — two people answering different questions with the same words, each convinced the other has simply got the facts wrong. The scientist and the cook were never really in conflict; they only sounded as though they were, because the single short word "fruit" was quietly doing two different jobs at once. Learn to ask what work a word is being made to do, and a surprising number of stubborn arguments reveal themselves to be misunderstandings dressed up as disagreements. The tomato is only the friendliest and most edible teacher of a lesson that reaches a good deal further than the kitchen.

The tomato, in short, is a small red lesson in a large idea: that truth is often relative to the question, and that two honest experts can disagree completely without either being wrong, so long as they are answering different questions with the same word.

It is a strangely comforting thought to end on. So much of what looks like conflict in the world is the tomato's predicament in disguise — a single word stretched across two purposes, two people each certain of a truth that the other has never actually denied. The scientist was right; the cook was right; the Supreme Court was right to side with the cook for the narrow purpose of a tax while conceding every point to the scientist. Nobody in the whole long story was ever mistaken about a fact. They were only, now and then, mistaken about which question was on the table. And that, perhaps, is the most useful thing a tomato can teach: before you argue about the answer, make quite sure you agree about the question.

So the next time someone puts the old question to you with that easy, unsuspecting confidence — is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? — you need not choose a side, and you certainly need not apologise. You are entitled instead to smile, perhaps to tell the tale of the Nix brothers and their crates of duty at the port of New York, and to give the only reply that is wholly and unimpeachably true: it depends, entirely and forever, on who you ask.
