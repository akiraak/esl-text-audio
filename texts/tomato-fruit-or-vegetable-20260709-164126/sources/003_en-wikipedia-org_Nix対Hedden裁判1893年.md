**Year & Parties:** Decided May 10, 1893, the case involved John Nix and his brothers against Edward L. Hedden, Collector of the Port of New York.

**The Tariff Issue:** President Chester A. Arthur signed the Tariff Act of 1883 on March 3, 1883, which required "a tax to be paid on imported vegetables, but not fruit." The Nix company challenged this by importing tomatoes and arguing they should qualify as duty-free fruit.

**Justice Horace Gray's Opinion:** Writing for a unanimous Court, Gray held that "The passages cited from the dictionaries define the word 'fruit' as the seed of plants... These definitions have no tendency to show that tomatoes are 'fruit,' as distinguished from 'vegetables,' in common speech."

**The Ruling:** The Court unanimously decided tomatoes must be classified as vegetables for tariff purposes based on "common parlance" rather than botanical classification—they were "usually eaten as a main course instead of being eaten as a dessert."

**Related Vegetables:** Gray similarly clarified the status of cucumbers, squashes, peas, and beans, treating them all according to common usage rather than strict botanical definitions.

(補足: 一般に知られる事実として、この裁判は関税法上の分類に関するものであり、植物学上トマトが果実であること自体を否定したものではない。裁判所は「日常語での意味」を採用したと説明している。)
