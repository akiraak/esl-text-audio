# Is a Tomato a Fruit or a Vegetable?

Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? Most people answer this question very quickly, but the real answer is not so simple. It depends on who you ask. A scientist and a cook look at the tomato in different ways, and surprisingly, both of them are right. This is the story of one small red food and two correct answers.

## What Science Calls a Fruit

Let's begin with science. Botany is the study of plants, and it has a clear rule for what a fruit is. In botany, a fruit is the part of a plant that grows from a flower and holds the seeds inside it. Its job is to keep the seeds safe. This rule does not care about taste. It does not matter if the food is sweet or not. If it grows from a flower and has seeds, science calls it a fruit.

## So the Tomato Is a Fruit

Now let's look at the tomato with this rule in mind. A tomato plant has small yellow flowers. The tomato grows right out of these flowers. If you cut a tomato open, you will see many seeds inside. So the tomato does everything the rule asks: it grows from a flower, and it holds seeds. For a scientist, then, the answer is clear. A tomato is a fruit.

## How Cooks Think

Cooks, however, look at food in a very different way. In the kitchen, people do not group food by flowers and seeds. They group it by taste and by how they use it. Some foods are sweet, and we eat them as a dessert. Other foods have a savory taste, and we use them in main dishes. This way of thinking is useful for chefs, for shoppers, and for people who plan meals, because foods that are close in science are not always close on the plate.

## The Tomato in the Kitchen

So how does the tomato fit into cooking? A tomato can be juicy, and you can eat it raw. But we almost never use it as a sweet dessert. Instead, we put it in salads, in soups, and in many other savory dishes, next to other vegetables. Because of the way we cook and eat it, chefs and shoppers call the tomato a vegetable. On the other hand, a scientist still calls it a fruit. Both people are looking at the same tomato, and both of them are right.

## Tomato Is Not Alone

The tomato may feel like a strange case, but it is not. Many foods are fruits in science and vegetables in the kitchen. Cucumbers, pumpkins, peppers, and eggplants all grow from flowers and hold seeds, so in botany they are fruits too. Yet we cook and eat them like vegetables. The surprise can also work the other way. Rhubarb and sweet potatoes are vegetables in science, but people sometimes use them in sweet, dessert-style dishes. So the line between "fruit" and "vegetable" moves depending on which rule you follow.

## The Court Decides

This question once became more than a game of words. It became a question about money. In 1893, the highest court in the United States had to decide the answer. Ten years earlier, in 1883, the country had a new tax law. Under this law, people paid a tax on imported vegetables, but not on imported fruit. So if a tomato was a fruit, it was cheaper. If it was a vegetable, it cost more. A company that brought in tomatoes did not want to pay, and the case went all the way to the court.

The judges knew that a tomato is a fruit in science. Even so, Justice Horace Gray, speaking for every judge, decided that in everyday language a tomato is a vegetable. He said that dictionaries define a fruit as the seed of a plant, but that this does not show how people really use the words. In common speech, he explained, a tomato is eaten with dinner, not served as a dessert. For this reason, the court called the tomato a vegetable.

## Both Answers Are Right

So, is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? The best answer is both. In science, it is a fruit, because it grows from a flower and holds seeds. In the kitchen, it is a vegetable, because of its savory taste and the way we use it. Neither answer is more "true" than the other. They simply come from two different ways of looking at the world. The next time someone asks you this question, you do not have to choose. You can smile and say, "It depends on who you ask."
