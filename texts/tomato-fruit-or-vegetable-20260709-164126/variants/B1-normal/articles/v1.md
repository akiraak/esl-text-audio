# Is a Tomato a Fruit or a Vegetable?

Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? Many people quickly answer, "A vegetable, of course." But this is not an easy question. The real answer depends on who you ask. A scientist and a cook may give you two different answers, and surprisingly, both of them are right.

Let's start with science. In botany, the study of plants, a fruit is the part of a plant that grows from a flower and holds the seeds. A tomato grows from small yellow flowers on the plant, and inside it you can find many seeds. For these reasons, a scientist will tell you that a tomato is a fruit. By this definition, the answer is clear.

Cooks, however, look at food in a different way. In the kitchen, people group food by its taste and by how they use it. We do not usually eat tomatoes as a sweet dessert. Instead, we put them in salads, soups, and other savory dishes, together with real vegetables. Because of this, a chef and most shoppers call the tomato a vegetable. On the other hand, a scientist still calls it a fruit. Both views make sense.

The tomato is not alone. Many foods that are fruits in science are used as vegetables in cooking. Cucumbers, pumpkins, peppers, and eggplants all grow from flowers and hold seeds, so in botany they are fruits too. But in the kitchen, we treat them like vegetables. So the tomato is not a strange case. It is a very common one.

This question even reached a court. In 1893, the highest court in the United States had to decide the answer. At that time, people paid a tax on imported vegetables, but not on fruit. So the difference was about money, not only about words. The court knew that a tomato is a fruit in science. Even so, the judges decided that, in everyday language, a tomato is a vegetable, because people eat it with dinner and not as dessert.

So, is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? The best answer is both. In science, it is a fruit. In the kitchen, it is a vegetable. The next time someone asks you this question, you can smile and say, "It depends on who you ask."
