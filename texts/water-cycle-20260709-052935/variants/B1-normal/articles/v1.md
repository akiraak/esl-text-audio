# The Water Cycle: A Journey That Never Ends

Have you ever watched rain fall and wondered where it comes from? The water on our planet never stays still. It moves in a great circle called the water cycle. This cycle has been running for millions of years, and it never stops.

The journey starts with the sun. Heat from the sun warms the oceans, lakes, and rivers. When water gets warm enough, it changes from a liquid into a gas. This process is called evaporation. Trees and plants also send water into the air. This is called transpiration. Every day, huge amounts of water rise into the sky, even though we cannot see it happen.

As the water vapor rises higher, the air around it becomes cooler. Cool air cannot hold as much water vapor as warm air, so the vapor changes back into tiny drops of liquid water. This process is called condensation, and it is how clouds are formed. If you have ever seen a cloud, you have seen millions of these tiny water drops floating together.

When a cloud becomes full of water, it cannot hold any more. The water falls back to Earth as rain, snow, or hail. This is called precipitation. Gravity pulls the water down from the sky to the ground.

Once the water reaches the ground, it does not stay in one place. Some of it flows over the land and runs into rivers and streams. Some of it slowly sinks into the soil and becomes groundwater, which is stored underground. This groundwater can later come back to the surface and flow into rivers again.

Almost all of this water eventually reaches the ocean, where the whole process begins again. In fact, the oceans store about 96 percent of all the water on Earth. Because of this, the ocean is the biggest starting point and the biggest ending point of the water cycle.

The water cycle may seem simple, but it is one of the most important processes on our planet. Without it, rain could not fall, rivers could not flow, and plants and animals could not survive. Every glass of water we drink today has traveled through this endless cycle many times before, moving between the sky, the land, and the sea.
