# Classroom Talk: Is a Tomato a Fruit or a Vegetable?

Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? In Ms. Rivera's English class, one small snack starts a big discussion. Three students take sides, and their teacher guides them through science, cooking, and even an old court case from 1893. By the end of the lesson, the class learns that some questions have two right answers.

## A Question at Snack Time

**Ms. Rivera:** Okay, everyone, break time is over. Please take your seats.

**Kenta:** One second, Ms. Rivera. I'm still finishing my snack.

**Maria:** Kenta, why are you eating fruit for a snack? Where is your chocolate?

**Kenta:** This is not fruit, Maria. A tomato is a vegetable. Everybody knows that.

**Maria:** No, no. A tomato has seeds inside, so it's a fruit. My grandmother grows tomatoes, and she told me.

**Omar:** Did someone say snack? Now I'm listening.

**Ms. Rivera:** Actually, this is a wonderful question for our discussion today. Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? Let's talk about it in English, together.

## Team Fruit and Team Vegetable

**Ms. Rivera:** First, let's vote. Raise your hand if you think a tomato is a fruit.

**Maria:** Fruit! It can taste a little sweet, and it's full of seeds.

**Ms. Rivera:** Thank you, Maria. Now, who says it's a vegetable?

**Kenta:** Me. We put tomatoes in salads and soups, next to onions and carrots. That's a vegetable's job.

**Ms. Rivera:** And you, Omar? Which team are you on?

**Omar:** I'm on the team that eats it. Fruit or vegetable — if it's food, I'm happy.

**Ms. Rivera:** A very peaceful position. Now, here is the surprise: both teams have good reasons. Let me show you.

## What Science Says

**Ms. Rivera:** Let's start with science. Botany is the study of plants, and it has a clear rule. In botany, a fruit is the part of a plant that grows from a flower and holds the seeds.

**Kenta:** So the rule is about flowers and seeds? Not about taste?

**Ms. Rivera:** Exactly. Taste is not important for this rule. Now think about a tomato plant. It has small yellow flowers, and the tomatoes grow from those flowers. When you cut a tomato open, what do you find?

**Maria:** Seeds! Many little seeds. So science says a tomato is a fruit. Team Fruit wins!

**Kenta:** Wait, wait. We haven't talked about the kitchen yet.

**Omar:** I like this class. We talk about food, and people call it "studying."

## What Cooks Say

**Ms. Rivera:** Kenta is right — cooks see food in a different way. In the kitchen, nobody asks about flowers or seeds. People group food by its taste and by the way they use it.

**Kenta:** That's my point! Sweet things like apples become desserts. Tomatoes go into salads, soups, and pasta sauce.

**Ms. Rivera:** Exactly. A tomato is juicy, and you can eat it raw. But we usually put it in savory dishes — "savory" means food that tastes of salt, not sugar. That's why cooks and shoppers call it a vegetable.

**Omar:** It's true. I have never seen a tomato birthday cake. And believe me, I have seen many cakes.

**Maria:** Hmm. So in the kitchen, Team Vegetable wins. One game, two winners?

**Ms. Rivera:** Wait a moment, Maria. The story gets stranger.

## The Tomato Is Not Alone

**Ms. Rivera:** The tomato is not a lonely, special case. Cucumbers, pumpkins, peppers, and eggplants also grow from flowers and hold seeds.

**Kenta:** So for science, they are all fruits, too?

**Ms. Rivera:** Yes. But in the kitchen, we treat them all like vegetables.

**Maria:** A cucumber is a fruit?! My whole life was a lie!

**Ms. Rivera:** And it works the other way, too. Rhubarb and sweet potatoes are vegetables in science, but people sometimes use them in sweet, dessert-style dishes.

**Omar:** Sweet potato dessert is real. My aunt makes it. When I visit her, it disappears very fast.

## The Day a Court Decided

**Ms. Rivera:** Now, here is my favorite part of the story. More than a hundred years ago, this question went to the highest court in the United States.

**Maria:** A court? Judges in black clothes, talking about tomatoes?

**Ms. Rivera:** Exactly that. In 1883, the United States got a new tax law. Companies had to pay a tax on imported vegetables — vegetables from other countries — but not on imported fruit.

**Kenta:** Ah, I see. So if a tomato is a fruit, it costs less money.

**Ms. Rivera:** Right. A company owned by the Nix family brought tomatoes into New York. They said, "Tomatoes are fruits, so we don't have to pay." The fight went all the way to the Supreme Court, and in 1893 the judges made their decision.

**Omar:** Team Fruit or Team Vegetable? Don't stop there, Ms. Rivera!

**Ms. Rivera:** The judges did not say that science was wrong. But the judge who wrote the decision, Horace Gray, said the law should follow everyday language. In everyday language, people eat tomatoes with dinner, not as a dessert. So all the judges agreed: for the tax, a tomato is a vegetable.

**Maria:** So a question about money decided the tomato's name?! Poor tomato.

**Kenta:** Poor tomato? The court agreed with me! Team Vegetable: one, Team Fruit: zero.

## Two Right Answers

**Ms. Rivera:** Let's finish our game, then. Kenta, Maria — the true score is one to one. In a science class, a tomato is a fruit, because it grows from a flower and holds seeds. In the kitchen, it's a vegetable, because we use it in savory dishes.

**Maria:** So the answer changes when the question changes.

**Ms. Rivera:** Beautifully said. Both answers are right. You only need to know who is asking — a scientist or a cook.

**Kenta:** Okay, Maria. Let's shake hands. It's a tie. But I'm still putting tomatoes in my salad.

**Maria:** And I'm still telling my grandmother she was right.

**Ms. Rivera:** And you, Omar? After all this, what is a tomato for you?

**Omar:** Ms. Rivera, my system is simpler. In my lunch box, a tomato goes into one group only: "already gone."
