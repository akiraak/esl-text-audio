## Botanical Classification

Botanically, a tomato qualifies as a fruit because it "contains seeds and grow[s] from the flower of the tomato plant." The botanical definition specifies that "a 'fruit' is the seed-bearing product that grows from the ovary of a flowering plant."

## Culinary Classification

Culinarily, tomatoes function as vegetables. The culinary system bases classifications on "the way the plants are used and their flavour profiles." While tomatoes "can be juicy, sweet and enjoyed raw," they're typically prepared in savory dishes, leading to their common classification as vegetables in cooking contexts.

## Other Examples

The article identifies additional botanical fruits treated as culinary vegetables: "avocado, olives, pumpkin, tomato, sweecorn, courgette, cucumber, green peas, chili, aubergine." These foods demonstrate how botanical and culinary definitions diverge based on practical usage rather than botanical structure.

## Purpose of Dual Classification

The botanical definition serves scientific purposes like identifying varieties and understanding growth, while the culinary definition better serves "the general public, nutritionists and chefs" since botanically-related foods don't necessarily share similar nutritional compositions.
