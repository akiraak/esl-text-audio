# Is a Tomato a Fruit or a Vegetable?

Few questions sound as harmless as this one, and fewer still turn out to be quite so quietly treacherous. Put it to a room full of people — is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? — and most will answer at once, with the untroubled confidence of someone who has never had the smallest reason to doubt it. And yet the honest reply is not a word at all but a counter-question: who, precisely, is doing the asking? For a scientist and a cook will each examine the very same tomato and arrive at flatly opposite verdicts, and — here is the part that tends to unsettle people — both of them will be entirely, demonstrably right. What follows is the story of one small red thing, of the two truths that decline to cancel each other out, and of the improbable afternoon on which the disagreement was carried all the way to the Supreme Court of the United States.

## A Simple Question?

The trouble with the question is that it disguises its own difficulty. It has the shape of the sort of thing that ought to have a single, settled answer — the kind you might find printed upside down at the bottom of a quiz. Is a whale a fish? No. Is a spider an insect? No. Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? Here the machinery jams, because unlike those other cases the tomato does not sit on one side of a single line. It sits, quite comfortably, on both sides of two different lines drawn by two different kinds of expert for two entirely different purposes.

This is why the confident answers come so quickly and yet so often collide. The person who says "vegetable, obviously" is thinking of dinner; the person who says "fruit, actually" is remembering a half-forgotten fact from a biology lesson. Neither is wrong, but neither has noticed that they are answering different questions. The real interest of the tomato is not that it is hard to classify but that it is easy to classify twice, in two incompatible-seeming ways, each of them perfectly correct within its own world.

It helps to remember that there is no law of nature requiring food to be sorted in only one way. We are so used to a single set of familiar labels — fruit, vegetable, meat, grain — that we imagine they are read straight off the things themselves, as though every cucumber arrived in the world with an invisible tag already tied to it. But a category is not a property of an object; it is a tool that somebody built for a purpose, and when the purposes differ, the tools differ too. The same food may then fall into different boxes without anything about the food itself having changed in the slightest. To see how this plays out with the tomato, we need to look carefully at both worlds in turn — first the world of the scientist, then the world of the cook.

## What Science Calls a Fruit

Begin with science, since science at least promises to be tidy. Botany, the study of plants, takes no interest in what tastes agreeable or what belongs beside the roast. It asks instead where a thing comes from and what it is built to do. To a botanist, a fruit is the seed-bearing structure that develops from the ovary of a flowering plant — the part that grows out of the flower and carries the seeds within it. Its purpose is reproductive rather than gastronomic: it exists to shelter the seeds and, in the fullness of time, to help the parent plant disperse its offspring into the world.

Observe what this definition conspicuously declines to mention. It says nothing whatever about sweetness. It does not ask whether you would gladly eat the thing for pudding, nor whether it would look eccentric in a salad. Sugar, flavour, the company a food keeps at the table — all of this lies entirely outside the botanist's field of vision. The single question that counts is one of origin and structure: did it grow from a flower, and does it enclose seeds? If the answer is yes, then whatever else the thing may be, science files it — without hesitation and without apology — under *fruit*.

There is a reason the botanist insists on so narrow and unsentimental a rule. Classification, for the scientist, is not a game of intuition but a tool for understanding how plants are related, how they grow, and how they reproduce; it helps in identifying varieties and in grasping the biology of the plant itself. A definition anchored to something as fickle as taste would be useless for that work; sweetness varies with ripeness, with variety, with the season, and tells you nothing reliable about a plant's biology. Anchor the definition instead to the flower and the seed, and you have something stable to reason with — something that holds firm across every kitchen and every cuisine on earth, and that lets a botanist in one country understand exactly what a botanist in another is talking about. Precision of that kind is not pedantry; it is the very thing that makes the science work.

## So the Tomato Is a Fruit

Hold the tomato up to that rule and the verdict all but delivers itself. A tomato plant produces small yellow flowers, and it is precisely from these flowers that each tomato swells and reddens; the fruit grows directly out of the bloom, exactly as the definition requires. Slice one open and the case grows harder still to dispute. Inside you find not a single seed but a whole crowded multitude of them, bedded in their soft, wet chambers of pulp, every one a potential plant biding its time.

The juicy flesh we prize is, from the plant's own point of view, merely the packaging and the bribe — the sweet, conspicuous parcel designed to persuade an animal to carry the seeds away and leave them somewhere new. What we treat as food, the plant treats as a strategy for reproduction; the seeds are the point, and the fruit is the means.

So the tomato honours every clause of the definition, and it does so without a flicker of ambiguity. It springs from a flower; it harbours its many seeds; it is manifestly the seed-bearing product of a flowering plant. For a scientist, then, there is no genuine riddle here at all — no delicate borderline case to be argued long into the night. Measured against the botanical rule, the tomato is a fruit: plainly, structurally, and beyond any reasonable contradiction. Were that the whole of the matter, the tomato would scarcely deserve an essay. It is not the whole of the matter, and the reason it is not takes us out of the laboratory altogether.

## How Cooks Think

Step out of the laboratory and into the kitchen and the entire frame of the question shifts silently beneath your feet. Cooks are not indifferent to where their food comes from, but origin is simply not the axis on which their world turns. In the kitchen, food is sorted not by flower and seed but by flavour and by use — by how a thing actually conducts itself once it meets the knife, the heat, and the palate. What matters is taste and function: is this something sweet, to be dwelt upon as a dessert, or something savoury, to be built into the substantial part of the meal?

This is no arbitrary prejudice, no mere neglect of the biology textbook. The culinary way of sorting food earns its keep precisely because it answers questions the botanical scheme cannot so much as hear. It is the classification that truly serves chefs, shoppers, and nutritionists, because two foods may be near neighbours in botanical terms and yet share nothing at all on the plate — different flavours, different uses, different stations in the architecture of a meal. When you are deciding what to cook or what to carry home from the market, a common ancestry among flowers is of no earthly use to you; what you need to know is how the thing tastes and what can be done with it. On that question botany falls mute, and the cook's classification steps forward to answer.

It is worth adding that this culinary sorting is not the crude or unschooled cousin of the scientific one. It has its own logic, its own consistency, and its own long tradition of expertise. A chef who groups the tomato with the onion rather than the apple is making a judgement every bit as principled, in its own domain, as the botanist who does the reverse. The two are not rivals competing for a single throne; they are specialists tending to separate kingdoms, each sovereign within its own borders and each faintly baffled by the other's concerns.

The nutritionist, incidentally, has a particular stake in the cook's way of sorting things, and the reason is worth spelling out. Foods that are close relations in botanical terms do not necessarily resemble one another once they are on the plate; a shared descent from a flower is no guarantee of a shared nutritional makeup. Knowing that two foods are botanical cousins therefore tells you very little about what they will do for the body that eats them. This is precisely why the culinary classification — organised around taste, use, and the practical business of eating — serves the general public, the nutritionist, and the chef better than the botanical one ever could, for all the latter's admirable rigour. Each scheme is excellent at its own job and useless at the other's.

## The Tomato in the Kitchen

And by the cook's measure, the tomato settles firmly among the vegetables. It can, to be sure, be juicy and even mildly sweet, and nothing forbids you from eating one raw, out of the hand, as you might an apple. But that is not how the tomato earns its living. In everyday practice it is put to work in savoury dishes — sliced into salads, simmered into soups, cooked down into sauces, set alongside the onions and the peppers and all the other undisputed vegetables of the pot. It is a labourer in the main course, not the sweet flourish that closes the meal.

That, in the end, is why cooks, shoppers, and countless ordinary eaters have always called the tomato a vegetable and felt not the faintest twinge of error in doing so. They are not being careless, nor are they ignorant of some fact that would embarrass them. They are simply answering a different question from the botanist's. Where science asks how the tomato is *made*, the kitchen asks how the tomato is *used*; and by that wholly reasonable standard the tomato is a vegetable, exactly as surely as it is a fruit by the other. The two answers do not compete. They occupy different rooms in the same house, and there is no staircase between them on which they might meet to quarrel.

## Tomato Is Not Alone

It is easy to imagine that the tomato is some singular oddity, a lone troublemaker dragged out to make a clever point. It is nothing of the kind. The same amiable double life is led by a whole company of thoroughly familiar foods. Cucumbers, squash, peppers, aubergines, and green peas are every one of them fruits by the botanical reckoning — each grown from a flower and stocked with seeds — and yet each is treated, without a moment's hesitation, as a vegetable in the kitchen. The tomato is merely the most celebrated member of a large and entirely respectable club, the one whose name comes up whenever the argument is raised.

And the traffic, intriguingly, runs in both directions across this border. There are foods that are vegetables to the botanist yet behave, on the plate, like fruits. Rhubarb and sweet potato are vegetables by strict botanical classification, and yet both are cheerfully enlisted in sweet, dessert-style recipes — the rhubarb stewed with sugar into a tart pink compote and folded into pies, the sweet potato candied or spiced and served as though it had every right to the pudding course. The border, in other words, is not a one-way gate through which stray fruits drift down into the humbler ranks of the vegetables; it is a genuine two-way crossing, with botanical fruits doing the daily work of vegetables and botanical vegetables occasionally moonlighting as sweets.

Once you accept that there are two honest systems of classification rather than one master scheme with a few awkward exceptions, this two-way traffic stops looking like a paradox and starts looking like exactly what you should have expected. Different maps, drawn for different journeys, will naturally place the same town in different provinces — and some towns will even seem to migrate, depending on which map you happen to be holding. The tomato is not an anomaly in an otherwise orderly world. It is a particularly loud example of how the world was always going to look, the moment we agreed to describe it in more than one useful way at once.

## The Court Decides

Here the tale takes its most improbable turn, for this genial disagreement did not remain the property of dinner tables and biology classes. In the United States it was carried, with a certain absurd solemnity, all the way to the Supreme Court — because, as so often, it came down in the end to money. The Tariff Act of 1883, signed into law by President Chester A. Arthur on the third of March that year, required a tax to be paid on imported vegetables while permitting imported fruit to enter duty-free. In an instant the whole airy question of fruit-or-vegetable acquired a hard and unmistakable price. A family of importers named Nix, who brought tomatoes through the port of New York, had a splendid financial motive for insisting that their tomatoes were fruit — and so a matter of pennies per crate began its unlikely ascent through the courts.

The Nix brothers sued Edward L. Hedden, the collector of the port of New York, and on the tenth of May in 1893 the case reached its conclusion in an opinion written, for a unanimous Court, by Justice Horace Gray. His reasoning is a small monument to common sense, all the more charming for the solemnity of its setting. Gray freely granted that the passages cited from the dictionaries defined fruit as the seed of the plant — that the botanists, in other words, had their facts in perfect order, and he did not dispute the science for a moment. But those definitions, he pointed out, had no tendency to show that tomatoes were fruit rather than vegetables *in the ordinary language of daily life* — and it was ordinary language, not the biology textbook, in which the tariff law was written and meant to be read. In common speech, tomatoes were served at dinner as part of the main course, with the soup or the fish or the meat, and not, like fruits generally, as dessert; and so, for the plain purposes of the tariff, vegetables they would be deemed to be. The same reasoning, he noted, swept up cucumbers, squashes, peas, and beans, all of which the everyday tongue likewise counted among the vegetables, whatever the botanists in their laboratories might prefer.

The genius of the ruling lies in what it carefully refrained from doing. The Court did not presume to overturn the science; it did not declare that the tomato had somehow ceased, in nature, to be a fruit. It simply decided that, for the narrow legal question actually before it, the right authority was common usage rather than botanical strictness. The justices, robed and unanimous, thus put the full weight of the law behind precisely the truce that this whole story has been circling toward — a formal acknowledgement that the tomato may be one thing to the scientist and another to everyone standing in a grocery queue, and that both may be right at once.

## Both Answers Are Right

So we arrive, by way of flowers and seeds and simmering sauces and a nineteenth-century tax dispute, at the only conclusion the evidence will support. The tomato is a fruit to the botanist and a vegetable to the cook, and neither answer is a forgery of the other. They are two correct answers to two genuinely different questions — one about how the tomato comes into being, the other about how it is eaten — and no amount of argument will ever collapse them into a single tidy verdict, because the two questions were never asking the same thing to begin with.

There is something quietly liberating in accepting this. We are trained, almost from childhood, to believe that every question of fact has exactly one right answer, and that anyone who insists on two must be fudging or hedging. The tomato gently disproves the rule. It shows that a thing can belong wholeheartedly to two categories at once, provided the categories were built for different work, and that the whole appearance of contradiction can dissolve the moment you stop asking "which is it, really?" and start asking, of each classification in turn, what it is actually *for*.

The tomato, in short, is a small red lesson in a large idea: that truth is often relative to the question, and that two honest experts can disagree completely without either being wrong, so long as they are answering different questions with the same word. So the next time someone puts the old question to you with that easy, unsuspecting confidence — is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? — you need not choose a side, and you certainly need not apologise. You are entitled instead to smile, perhaps to mention the Nix brothers and their crates of tomatoes at the port of New York, and to give the only reply that is wholly and unimpeachably true: it depends on who you ask.
