# Why Honey Doesn't Spoil

## Historical Evidence
Archaeologists excavating ancient Egyptian tombs have discovered pots of honey thousands of years old that remain preserved and edible, demonstrating honey's remarkable longevity.

## Key Factors

**Low Water Content**
Honey is hygroscopic, meaning it naturally contains very little moisture. As explained by Amina Harris, executive director of the Honey and Pollination Center: "Very few bacteria or microorganisms can survive in an environment like that, they just die."

**High Acidity**
Honey has a pH between 3 and 4.5, which is extremely acidic. "That acid will kill off almost anything that wants to grow there," Harris notes, making the environment inhospitable to spoilage organisms.

**Hydrogen Peroxide**
Bees produce an enzyme called glucose oxidase in their stomachs. When regurgitating nectar into combs, this enzyme breaks down into gluconic acid and hydrogen peroxide. Harris explains that "hydrogen peroxide is the next thing that goes into work against all these other bad things that could possibly grow."

## Critical Storage Condition
The seal is essential. As Harris emphasizes: "As long as the lid stays on it and no water is added to it, honey will not go bad." Once exposed to moisture or air, honey can eventually spoil.
