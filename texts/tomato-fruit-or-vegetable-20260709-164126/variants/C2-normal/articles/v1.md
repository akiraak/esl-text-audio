# Is a Tomato a Fruit or a Vegetable?

Few questions sound as innocent as this one, and fewer still turn out to be so quietly treacherous. Ask a room full of people whether a tomato is a fruit or a vegetable, and most will answer at once, with the easy confidence of someone who has never had reason to doubt it. Yet the honest reply is not a word but a counter-question: who, exactly, is doing the asking? A botanist and a cook will look at the very same tomato and reach opposite verdicts, and — this is the part that unsettles people — both of them will be entirely right. What follows is the story of one small red thing and the two truths that refuse to cancel each other out.

## What Science Calls a Fruit

Begin, then, with science, because science at least promises to be tidy. Botany, the study of plants, does not concern itself with what tastes pleasant or what belongs beside the roast. It asks instead where a thing comes from and what it is built to do. To a botanist, a fruit is the seed-bearing structure that develops from the ovary of a flowering plant — the part that grows out of the flower and carries the seeds within it. Its purpose is reproductive rather than gastronomic: it exists to shelter the seeds and, in time, to help the plant scatter its offspring into the world.

Notice what this definition conspicuously ignores. It says nothing whatever about sweetness. It does not ask whether you would happily eat the thing for dessert, nor whether it would seem out of place in a salad. Sugar, flavour, the company a food keeps at the dinner table — all of this is simply beneath the botanist's notice. The single question that matters is one of origin and structure: did it grow from a flower, and does it hold seeds? If the answer is yes, then whatever else it may be, science files it, without hesitation and without apology, under *fruit*.

## So the Tomato Is a Fruit

Hold the tomato up to that rule and the verdict all but delivers itself. A tomato plant produces small yellow flowers, and it is precisely from these flowers that the tomato swells and ripens; the fruit grows directly out of the bloom, exactly as the definition demands. Slice one open and the case becomes still harder to dispute: inside you will find not one seed but a whole crowded multitude of them, suspended in their soft chambers of pulp, each one a potential plant in waiting.

So the tomato satisfies every condition the definition lays down, and it does so without the faintest ambiguity. It springs from a flower; it cradles its seeds; it is manifestly the seed-bearing product of a flowering plant. To a scientist, therefore, there is no genuine puzzle here at all, no borderline case to agonise over. Measured against the botanical rule, the tomato is a fruit — plainly, structurally, and beyond reasonable argument. If the story ended here, it would hardly be worth telling. It does not end here.

## How Cooks Think

Walk out of the laboratory and into the kitchen, however, and the whole frame of the question quietly shifts beneath your feet. Cooks are not indifferent to where food comes from, but it is not the question that governs their world. In the kitchen, food is sorted not by flower and seed but by flavour and by use — by how a thing actually behaves once it reaches the pan, the plate, and the palate. What matters is taste and role: is this something sweet, to be lingered over as a dessert, or is it something savoury, to be built into the main body of a meal?

This is no arbitrary prejudice, no mere failure to have read the botany textbook. The culinary way of sorting food earns its keep because it answers questions the botanical scheme cannot even hear. It is the classification that genuinely serves chefs, shoppers, and nutritionists, precisely because two foods can be close cousins in botanical terms and yet have nothing in common on the plate — different flavours, different uses, different places in the architecture of a meal. When you are planning what to cook and what to buy, a shared ancestry among flowers is of no earthly help; what you need to know is how the thing tastes and what you can do with it. On that question, botany falls silent, and the cook's classification steps in to speak.

## The Tomato in the Kitchen

And by the cook's measure, the tomato lands firmly among the vegetables. It can, admittedly, be juicy and even faintly sweet, and there is nothing to stop you eating one raw, out of hand, like an apple. But that is not how it earns its living. In practice the tomato is deployed in savoury dishes — sliced into salads, simmered into soups, folded into sauces, set to work alongside the onions and the peppers and the other undisputed vegetables of the kitchen. It is a supporting player in the main course, not the sweet flourish at the end of the meal.

That, in the end, is why cooks, shoppers, and countless ordinary eaters have always called the tomato a vegetable and felt not the slightest twinge of error in doing so. They are not being careless or ignorant; they are simply answering a different question from the botanist's. Where science asks how the tomato is *made*, the kitchen asks how the tomato is *used* — and by that entirely reasonable standard, the tomato is a vegetable, as surely as it is a fruit by the other.

It is worth pausing to notice, too, that the tomato is no lonely exception, no freak case dragged out to make a clever point. The same amiable double life is led by a whole company of familiar foods. Cucumbers, squash, peppers, and aubergines are all, botanically speaking, fruits — each grown from a flower and stocked with seeds — and yet every one of them is treated, without a second thought, as a vegetable in the kitchen. The tomato is merely the most famous member of a large and thoroughly respectable club. And the traffic occasionally runs the other way: rhubarb and sweet potato are vegetables by the botanical reckoning, yet both are cheerfully pressed into service in sweet, dessert-style dishes. Once you accept that there are two honest systems of classification rather than one, you should expect a certain amount of good-natured wandering across the border.

## The Court Decides

Here the tale takes its most improbable turn, for this genial disagreement did not stay confined to dinner tables and biology classes. In the United States it went all the way to the Supreme Court — because, as so often, it came down to money. The Tariff Act of 1883, signed into law by President Chester A. Arthur on the third of March that year, required a tax to be paid on imported vegetables while allowing imported fruit to enter duty-free. Suddenly the whole airy question of fruit-or-vegetable acquired a very concrete price tag, and a family of importers named Nix, who brought tomatoes into the port of New York, had an excellent financial reason to insist that their tomatoes were fruit. They sued the port's tax collector, Edward Hedden, and the quarrel climbed, with a certain absurd dignity, to the highest court in the land.

In 1893 the Court delivered its unanimous answer, in an opinion written by Justice Horace Gray, and its reasoning is a small masterpiece of common sense. Gray readily conceded that the dictionaries defined fruit as the seed of the plant — that the botanists, in other words, had their facts perfectly straight. But those definitions, he observed, did nothing to show that tomatoes were fruit rather than vegetables *in the ordinary language of everyday life*. In common speech, tomatoes were served as part of the main course and not as dessert, and so, for the plain purposes of the tariff, vegetables they would be. The same treatment, he noted, applied to cucumbers, squashes, peas, and beans, which the everyday tongue likewise counted as vegetables whatever the botanists might say.

The Court, it should be stressed, did not presume to overturn the science; it did not declare that the tomato had ceased, in nature, to be a fruit. It simply chose, for the narrow question before it, to be ruled by common usage rather than by botanical strictness. And in doing so it laid down, with the full solemnity of the law, exactly the truce that this whole story has been circling toward. The tomato is a fruit to the scientist and a vegetable to the cook, and neither answer is the counterfeit of the other; they are two right answers to two different questions. So the next time someone puts the question to you with that easy, unsuspecting confidence, you are entitled to smile, and to give the only reply that is wholly true: it depends on who you ask.
