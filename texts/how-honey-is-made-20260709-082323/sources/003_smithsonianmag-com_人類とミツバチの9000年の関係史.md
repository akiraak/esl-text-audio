# History of Humans and Honeybees

**Earliest Evidence:**
The oldest archaeological evidence dates to 7,000 B.C. in Anatolia (Asia Minor), specifically at the Stone Age site Çayönü Tepesi in southeastern Turkey, which yielded "exceptionally well-preserved beeswax residue."

**Rock Art Documentation:**
A honey-hunting scene appears in a Valencia, Spain rock painting from approximately 6,000 B.C., depicting a person raiding a hive.

**Ancient Egypt:**
Cylindrical hives are documented in Egyptian tomb images "dating as far back as the 7th century B.C." Pharaohs held the title "Bee King" due to extensive Lower Egypt beekeeping operations.

**Neolithic Pottery Evidence:**
Researchers analyzed over 6,400 pottery pieces for beeswax residue. Key geographic findings include:
- **Balkans**: 5,500–4,500 B.C.
- **North Africa**: 5,000 B.C.
- **Denmark**: The northernmost location, representing "the ecological limit of honeybees in prehistory"

**Research Method:**
Scientists identified beeswax through its distinctive chemical composition, as it "withstands the ravages of time" better than physical bee remains.

Human-bee relationships evolved from wild honey gathering to eventual domestication and formal beekeeping practices.
