## Botanical Classification (Fruit)

Tomatoes are scientifically classified as fruits because they "form from small yellow flowers on the vine and naturally contain a multitude of seeds." The botanical definition requires that fruits develop from a flower's ovary and contain seeds for reproduction.

## Culinary Classification (Vegetable)

In cooking, tomatoes function as vegetables due to their savory applications. They are "usually used alone or paired alongside other true vegetables in savory dishes," which has given them a vegetable reputation despite their botanical status.

## Other Examples

The article notes that several botanically-classified fruits are commonly used as vegetables in cooking, including:
- Cucumber
- Squash
- Pea pods
- Peppers
- Eggplant
- Okra

Interestingly, the reverse occasionally occurs—rhubarb and sweet potato are vegetables botanically but are "often included in sweet dessert-style recipes."

## Historical Note

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in 1893 that tomatoes should be classified as vegetables "on the basis of its culinary applications" rather than botanical science, establishing legal precedent for their vegetable classification.
